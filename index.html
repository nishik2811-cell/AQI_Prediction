01 — What this project does

From a raw sensor feed to a next-hour forecast

Five stages, run in order, turn a noisy CSV of hourly pollutant readings into a working predictor of Delhi's Air Quality Index.

01

Diagnose the sensors

Check every column for missing values, duplicate timestamps, and physically impossible negative readings before touching anything.

co · no · no2 · o3 · so2pm2.5 · pm10 · nh3
02

Fill the time gaps

Reindex to a strict one-reading-per-hour grid and interpolate across any hour the sensors missed, so every downstream step sees a continuous timeline.

03

Correct anomalies

Cap statistical outliers, unfreeze sensors that got stuck repeating one value, and fix ozone readings that stayed implausibly high overnight — when there's no sunlight left to produce ozone.

04

Engineer the features

Add time-of-day and season as smooth cyclical signals, plus lagged and rolling pollutant history — using only what would have been known at that hour, with no peeking at the future.

05

Compute AQI and train

Derive the official AQI from PM2.5 and PM10 sub-indices, then train a Random Forest on a chronological split — tested only on hours the model never trained on.

AQI is derived from PM2.5 and PM10 sub-indices on the Indian CPCB scale (0–500). The model is a Random Forest trained on lagged pollutant history, rolling averages, and cyclical time features, split chronologically so the test set is genuinely unseen future data.
